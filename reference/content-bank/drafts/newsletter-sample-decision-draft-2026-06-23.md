## Draft

Kia ora,

One of the small jobs in the workshop this week was making custom timber samples for a dining table decision.

That sounds simple, but it is often one of the most useful parts of the process.

A screen can show grain and colour roughly. A showroom table can help. A photo from another project can point things in the right direction. But timber changes once it is cut, sanded, finished and seen in the light of the actual home.

Even the same finish can behave differently across different timbers.

A pale oil that softens one species can make another look warmer. A timber that looks calm unfinished can become stronger once the finish goes on. Sometimes the sample confirms the first idea. Sometimes it quietly rules it out.

That is the point.

A dining table is too large, too central and too long-lived to choose from a guess if the colour matters. A sample gives the decision something real to sit on: the species, the finish, the tone, and how it feels next to the room around it.

It is not about making the process complicated. It is about removing uncertainty before the full table is made.

If you are choosing timber for a table, especially between close options, ask to see a sample in your own space. The best choice is often much easier once the timber is in your hand.

Ngā mihi,

Guido and the Innate team
