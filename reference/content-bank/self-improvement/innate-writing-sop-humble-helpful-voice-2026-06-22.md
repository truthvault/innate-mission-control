# Innate writing SOP: humble, helpful, simple voice

Date: 2026-06-22
Owner: Content profile
Status: active draft standard, to refine with Guido feedback
Scope: Innate newsletters, social captions, project stories, approval packs, website copy and partner story drafts

## Why this exists

The Stadium story exposed a core failure: the copy kept reaching for significance before earning it.

Two failed openings:

1. Grand civic framing: “the scale, the roof, the seating, the city stepping into a new chapter.”
2. Reader-diminishing framing: “most people will never stop to think about...”

Both fail for different reasons.

The first makes Innate sound like it is trying to attach itself to something bigger than its real role.

The second makes the reader feel ordinary or inattentive, as if the writer is smarter than them for noticing the detail.

Innate cannot write like that.

## Innate writing north star

Innate should sound like:

> A practical Christchurch workshop that knows timber, respects the reader, explains choices plainly, and lets the work carry the pride.

The reader should feel:

- included, not lectured;
- capable, not ignorant;
- trusted, not manipulated;
- quietly impressed by the work, not by the copy;
- invited closer to the material, not pushed toward a sale.

## The reader-respect rule

Before any line goes into a draft, ask:

> Does this line make the reader feel more capable, more included or more curious?

If it makes them feel:

- ordinary in a bad way;
- inattentive;
- naive;
- outside the clever insight;
- pressured to admire the brand;
- like they are being taught a moral lesson,

rewrite it.

## Start with the strongest relationship, not the isolated object

For public project stories, especially commercial or civic work, the first question is not “what object did Innate make?”

The first question is:

> What relationship made this work possible, and what did each party bring?

Innate is not an ice-cream stand and should not write like a simple product caption when the real value is collaboration, trust, procurement, specification and delivery.

For partner-led projects, the default hierarchy is:

1. The partnership or collaboration that made the work possible.
2. The shared brief, trust or project need.
3. The practical role each partner played.
4. The object/material/workshop response.
5. The wider local/provenance story, only after the relationship is clear.

Good Innate openings may begin with:

1. The partner relationship.
2. The collaboration or procurement path.
3. The shared brief/problem.
4. The trust required to bring a local workshop into a larger project.
5. The object in use, when the project is object-led rather than relationship-led.
6. The material, when provenance is the main story.
7. The workshop decision, when the image/process is the source.

Avoid opening with:

- the grand meaning of the project;
- an isolated object when the stronger story is collaboration;
- what “most people” notice or fail to notice;
- abstract local pride;
- a claim that the project “shows”, “reflects”, “represents” or “symbolises” something;
- a product-caption structure when the work is really a relationship story.

## The Innate paragraph test

Each paragraph should do at least one real job:

- name a real thing;
- explain a real decision;
- reduce a reader’s uncertainty;
- show how the work was done;
- clarify the role of a partner or material;
- connect the object to its use;
- ask for a narrow approval or next step.

If a paragraph only creates mood, delete or rewrite it.

## Anti-patterns to ban

### 1. Grand civic inflation

Bad:

> The scale, the roof, the seating, the feeling of a city stepping into a new chapter.

Why it fails:

- Sounds like stadium marketing.
- Makes Innate sound like it is borrowing importance.
- Pushes the story bigger before earning it.

Use instead:

- Start with the supplied piece, the material, the brief or the service setting.

### 2. Reader-diminishing insight

Bad:

> Most people will never stop to think about the serving board under the food.

Why it fails:

- Makes the reader feel like one of the inattentive “most people.”
- Creates a superiority gap between writer and reader.
- Turns the brand into the clever observer rather than the helpful maker.

Use instead:

- Invite the reader into a detail without making them wrong for not already noticing it.

### 3. Fake humility

Bad:

> They are not the loudest part of the building. They are not meant to be.

Why it can fail:

- Can sound written and self-conscious.
- Still centres the cleverness of the copy.

Use carefully, or replace with a practical line.

### 4. Abstract significance language

Avoid:

- reflects;
- represents;
- symbolises;
- underscores;
- showcases;
- pivotal;
- meaningful guest experience, unless quoting or specifically evidenced;
- the heart of the story;
- small choices inside a large civic project, unless grounded first.

### 5. Over-polished rule of three

Avoid neat rhythm that sounds assembled:

> local food, regional suppliers and the identity of Canterbury

Sometimes this is accurate, but too much of it turns into brochure cadence. Use sparingly.

### 6. Brand-as-hero framing

Avoid making Innate the hero.

Better hierarchy for partner projects:

1. The customer / venue / partner need.
2. The object or material response.
3. The practical role Innate played.
4. The wider story only if the facts support it.

## Positive patterns

### Pattern 1: Helpful observation

Formula:

> [Specific object/material/use]. [Plain reason it matters]. [How Innate handled it].

Example shape:

> A serving board in a hospitality space has a practical job before it has a story. It needs to be stable, cleanable, consistent across a batch, and good enough to sit under food that has had real care put into it.

Why it works:

- Starts with use.
- Respects the reader.
- Lets the story emerge.

### Pattern 2: Reader-capable explanation

Formula:

> If you are choosing / specifying / looking at [thing], here is the useful distinction.

Example shape:

> For commercial hospitality pieces, the question is not only whether the timber looks good on day one. It is whether the piece can be repeated, handled, cleaned, repaired and still feel right in the room.

Why it works:

- Makes the reader smarter.
- No superiority.
- Shows judgement.

### Pattern 3: Partner-respect framing

Formula:

> [Partner] had the brief/context. [Innate] made the timber response. [Approval-sensitive claim stays cautious].

Example shape:

> Aitkens understood the hospitality brief and brought Innate into the timber pieces where local making made sense.

Why it works:

- Does not reduce the partner to a middleman.
- Does not overclaim Innate’s role.

### Pattern 4: Plain provenance

Formula:

> [Material]. [Place]. [Why it was suitable]. [What was done with it].

Example shape:

> The pieces were made from West Coast beech in our Christchurch workshop. Beech suited this job because it is clean, practical and hard-working without feeling visually heavy.

Why it works:

- Specific.
- Useful.
- Calm.

### Pattern 5: Testimonial as story spine

Formula:

> [Partner says what matters]. [Story follows that logic]. [Innate details prove it rather than distracting from it].

Example shape:

> Daniel Shanks’ testimonial is not a decorative quote. It names the spine of the Stadium story: local producers, aligned values, West Coast beech, authentic sense of place, regional food, craftsmanship, sustainability, Aotearoa identity, local economy, community, and meaningful guest experience. The draft should follow that logic before introducing extra Innate-led angles.

Why it works:

- Treats partner language as evidence.
- Keeps the collaboration central.
- Prevents material facts from becoming isolated product details.

## Drafting checklist before showing Guido

Run these checks manually or with a script:

1. No em dash characters.
2. No grand civic opening unless explicitly requested.
3. No “most people don’t notice / think / understand” framing.
4. No “not just X, but Y” formula unless unavoidable.
5. No luxury, stunning, elevate, showcase, pivotal, testament, iconic, world-class fluff.
6. First paragraph starts with a real Innate detail, not abstract meaning.
7. Every paragraph makes the reader more capable, more included or more curious.
8. Partner roles are respectful and permission-safe.
9. Claims are grounded in source notes or clearly marked for approval.
10. Read aloud. If it sounds like a press release, brand agency, LinkedIn thought leader or museum plaque, rewrite.

## Correction protocol

When Guido says a draft sounds wrong:

1. Stop generating variants.
2. Name the failure mode in plain language.
3. Remove or quarantine the bad copy from active drafts.
4. Update this SOP or the marketing skill if the rule should persist.
5. Ask one focused question if more calibration is needed.
6. Do not resume drafting until the rule is applied.

## Tool / skill gap assessment

The missing piece is not a paid writing tool.

Current tools are enough for world-class drafting if used properly:

- local source search;
- session search;
- web research;
- brand examples;
- humanizer skill;
- durable skill/reference patching;
- correction logs;
- eval checklists.

The failure was process, not tooling:

- I wrote before fully applying reader-respect and humanizer gates.
- I overcorrected from grand to “clever observer”, which still centred the writer instead of the reader.
- The skill lacked a sharp enough rule for reader dignity.

Needed operating change:

- Before any serious Innate draft, run the reader-respect test and the anti-AI significance filter.
- For uncertain tone, ask Guido one question before writing another version.
